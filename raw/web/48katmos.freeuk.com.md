## What's an Oric Atmos — http://www.48katmos.freeuk.com/whatisan.htm

For those that don't know, the Oric is an 8-bit computer from the eighties. Originally there was the black and grey Oric-1. This was upgraded to become the Oric Atmos.

The following was taken from a sales brochure released to promote the Atmos system, comprising of Atmos computer, Oric Microdisc and MCP40 printer plotter all in Atmos livery.

THE ORIC ATMOS 48K — The new computer that takes a remarkable step forward in home computer technology. Which is hardly surprising when you consider it's a development of the Oric-1, the UK computer that was voted 'Best of the Year' in France. The original best-selling Oric-1 (150,000 already sold) has been completely re-styled, and refined to include a number of important advances.

USABLE MEMORY — Unlike other home computers it uses the highly sophisticated serial attribute handling method used by Viewdata and Teletext. So these attributes are stored on the screen alongside the data, instead of taking up storage space in the memory. Thus the Oric Atmos never offers less than a healthy 37K of usable memory — even when the new colour printer and disc drive unit are attached.

COLOURS — The Atmos offers 8 for graphics and text and 8 background colours. You can also 'paint over' the graphics area and mix text freely with colours and shapes.

SOUND — Most other computers don't have speakers and rely on a television set to produce the sound. The Atmos gives you a powerful loudspeaker and internal amplifier — which can be connected to your hi-fi system to produce sound quality on a par with the £400 BBC Micro. The Atmos measures up to the most sophisticated machines, and is even able to synthesize the sound of musical instruments.

KEYBOARD — One important advance on the original Oric is the professional keyboard. The Atmos now gives you a full response 'typewriter style' keyboard.

LANGUAGE — The Oric uses an extended version of Microsoft BASIC.

DESIGN — The new Oric Atmos includes many special features: ports for peripherals, text mode of 28 rows by 40 columns, graphics resolution of 200 points by 240 points, compatible with most domestic cassette players, warm reset control, comes with all leads needed.

Commodore tried to sue Oric for failing to mention that you could free some of the C64's memory. Oric responded by saying they weren't running that advertising campaign any more so it was irrelevant.

## Atmos Specifications — http://www.48katmos.freeuk.com/techatmo.htm

Oric offer 16K and 48K Atmos models, both featuring 'typewriter' style keyboards.

**Processor:** 6502A Microprocessor with 16K ROM containing BASIC interpreter and operating system. RAM: 16K or 48K Byte. 48K model contains 64K of RAM with 16K overlaid by ROM.

**Keyboard:** 57 moving keys with tactile feedback. Full upper and lower case with correctly positioned space bar. Full typewriter pitch. All keys have auto repeat.

**Display — Character Mode:** 28 lines of 40 characters (Teletext-like). Character set is standard ASCII enhanced by 80 user definable characters. Serial attributes control display features: background colour (8 choices), foreground colour (8 choices), flash, double height, user-defined characterset. Available colours: Black, Blue, Red, Magenta, Green, Cyan, Yellow, White. Each character position also has a parallel attribute for video inversion.

**Display — Graphics Mode:** 200 pixels vertically by 240 pixels horizontally plus 3 lines of 40 characters at bottom. Graphics display operates with serial attributes. ASCII characters may be 'painted over' the graphics area enabling free mixing of graphics and text.

**Sound:** Internal loudspeaker and amplifier; connectable to external Hi-Fi via DIN connector. Three-channel sound synthesizer (General Instrument 8912). Pseudo Random Noise generator. Sound commands: PING, SHOOT, EXPLODE, ZAP. General purpose commands: SOUND (15Hz–62kHz), MUSIC (seven octave range, up to 3 voices), PLAY.

**Cassette Interface:** DIN socket. Tangerine format at 300 baud or 2400 baud. Schmitt trigger noise removal. Commands: CLOAD, CSAVE, STORE, RECALL. Remote motor control.

**Expansion Port:** Full data, address and control information for the 6502A Microprocessor. RAM and ROM externally expandable.

**Printer Port:** Standard Centronics parallel interface (20-pin IDC).

**Reset:** Warm start via key on base of machine — returns to command mode without destroying program or data.

**BASIC:** Extended Microsoft BASIC. Mathematical functions: sine, cosine, tangent, arctan, exponential, log, absolute value, integer, sign, square root. String operations: ASC, CHR$, LEFT$, LEN, MID$, RIGHT$, TAB, VAL. Variables: any length (first two chars acted upon), integer (%), float, string ($). Arrays: up to 255 dimensions.

**Dimensions:** Height: 52mm, Width: 280mm, Depth: 175mm, Weight: 1.1Kg

Note: More BASIC commands available when using Sedoric.

## The Oric-1 Page — http://www.48katmos.freeuk.com/oric1.htm

Before the Oric, Tangerine Computer Systems Ltd designed computers. The most successful was the monochrome Microtan 65, supplied in kit form. Tangerine's success prompted a colour home computer. Oric Products International Ltd was set up to produce it.

The original design (Tangerine Tiger) was sold off. Microtan 2 with Prestel capability became the basis for the Oric. The original design was reportedly drawn out on the back of a napkin; a breadboard was built to test the circuitry, then a prototype shown Aug/Sept 1982.

The Oric-1 was introduced as the first colour computer under £100 (16K version). Both 16K and 48K versions were released but problems arose and some machines had to be returned. Various board versions were released:

- **PCB Issue 1** — rarest, possibly pre-release
- **PCB Issue 2** — rare, seen only in magazine articles
- **PCB Issue 3** — most common board, used in Oric-1s and some Atmoses; came in semi-transparent and opaque variants
- **PCB Issue 4** — later board with printed EPROM labels; both issue 3 and 4 were used in Atmos

Early models used two 2764 EPROMs (Hitachi, hand-written labels 'H' and 'L') and required chip IC11. Later models used Mitsubishi EPROMs with printed labels. Then came production ROMs (single chip by Hitachi, twice the capacity, making IC11 redundant).

Logo variants: grey version (most common on Oric-1) and colour RGB version — neither is particularly rare.

## Oric ROMs — http://www.48katmos.freeuk.com/roms.htm

ROMs store the operating system and BASIC. Oric games come on cassette or disk — no games ROMs.

**Oric-1 ROMs:**
- Early models: two 2764 EPROMs (Hitachi, hand-written labels 'H'/'L'); required chip IC11
- Later models: two Mitsubishi EPROMs with printed labels
- Production ROMs: single Hitachi chip (twice the capacity, IC11 no longer needed)
- V1.0 ROM: same content as the two EPROMs, single chip

**Atmos ROMs:**
- Atmos V1.1 ROM: similar to Oric-1 but with many bugs removed. Two variants:
  - Earlier version: PEEK(#E4B6) = 162
  - Later version: PEEK(#E4B6) = 142 — cassette loading routine modified to reduce 'Errors Found' messages
- Rockwell version of the later Atmos ROM (number 1815)
- Oric-1 ROMs give PEEK(#E4B6) = 48

Also mentioned: Rhetoric Oric Meet — a rare special ROM.

## The Back End — Oric Port Info — http://www.48katmos.freeuk.com/ports.htm

**TV connection:** PAL UHF (channel 36). Phono plug on Oric side, coaxial to standard TV plug.

**RGB monitor connection:** 5-pin DIN (270° configuration).
- Pin 1: Red, Pin 2: Green, Pin 3: Blue, Pin 4: SYNC, Pin 5: Ground
- Can connect to SCART/RGB/composite video via screened cable

**Cassette & Sound port:** DIN plug with 3 jack plugs option.
- Pin 1: Tape Out, Pin 2: Ground, Pin 3: Tape In, Pin 4: Sound, Pin 5: Sound, Pin 6/7: Relay Contacts (cassette motor control)
- Can double as Hi-Fi sound output

**Centronics Printer Port:** 20-pin IDC plug (not standard Centronics D-connector). 8 data lines, Strobe, ACK, plus grounds. Custom cable required. The 6522 chip is shared between sound and printer port, causing sound effects during joystick use via printer port.

**BUS Expansion port:** Full 6502A data/address/control lines, 34-pin connector. Most commonly used for disk interface. Pin mapping documented (addresses A0–A15, data D0–D7, control lines MAP, 02, ROMDIS, RESET, I/O, R/W, IRQ, +5V, GND).

**No joystick ports:** joysticks added via expansion port or printer port. No RS232 — Centronics parallel only (an RS232 adapter for the expansion bus allows modem connection).

**Power:** 9V DC centre-positive, 5.4VA original. 1A supplies work. Original Spectrum PSU can be used with polarity reversed — do NOT use 128K Spectrum PSU.

## Support for Oric Users — http://www.48katmos.freeuk.com/support.htm

**Rhetoric magazine (by Steve Marshall):**
Rhetoric was a print magazine set up to support English-speaking Oric fans, continuing where Oric User Monthly (OUM) left off. Founded by Steve Marshall, Simon Ullyatt (editor), Jon Bristow, Jim Groom, Matt Coates, and Brian Kidd. Produced 25 issues over ~3 years plus 10 software disks. Final issue (#25) eventually sent to remaining subscribers.

OUM was founded by Dave Dick, providing software/hardware sales, support, and a monthly magazine. Rhetoric aimed to continue under a new name to encourage new contributors.

Club Europe Oric (CEO) continues to produce a magazine and disk.
