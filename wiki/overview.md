# Oric retro computer — overview

A recurring theme in this wiki is keeping the 1980s Tangerine Oric-1 and Oric Atmos alive through modern, open-hardware PCB projects. [[OldWer-Metaphoric]] documents Metaphoric, a full Oric clone distributed as KiCAD board designs — it reimplements the machine on a two-PCB main+keyboard set, with its memory subsystem derived from the earlier OriClone-1 project, and adds modern conveniences such as composite video, flexible power and dual joystick ports while letting one build switch between Oric-1 and Atmos ROMs.

[[Board-Folk-Oric-Remix]] takes the complementary approach: rather than a clone, it is a faithful replica of the original Oric motherboard that stays ROM-, software- and form-factor-compatible and still requires the original core chips, including the ULA. It is aimed at repairing or rebuilding genuine Oric machines, folding in service-manual tape modifications, a corrected /ROMDIS circuit, AD724 composite video and an optional Commodore 1531 tape interface. Together the two sources frame the practical choice for a hobbyist today — build a modernised clone from scratch ([[OldWer-Metaphoric]]) or remanufacture an authentic board to revive existing hardware ([[Board-Folk-Oric-Remix]]).

Underpinning both of those projects is the original machine itself, and [[oric.free.fr]] is the wiki's primary reference for it. A static Oric resource site online since 1998, maintained by Euphoric emulator author Fabrice Frances, it supplies the two things the hardware projects assume but do not themselves document: a register-level hardware-programming manual for the Oric's 6502/VIA/UAL/PSG architecture, and a full narrative history of Oric Products International — its 1983 launch, chronic over-optimism, twin receiverships, and the French-led afterlife that produced Sedoric and the Telestrat. It also archives the surviving software, emulator and tool ecosystem. Read it to understand what [[OldWer-Metaphoric]] and [[Board-Folk-Oric-Remix]] are faithfully reproducing.

Where [[oric.free.fr]] documents the original machine, [[defence-force.org]] covers the question of how new Oric software is written today. The personal site of developer Mickaël Pointier, online since 1997, it is the home of the OSDK (Oric Software Development Kit) — a modern PC-hosted cross-development toolchain with a C compiler, 6502 assembler, source-level linker and a full set of tape/disk-image and asset-conversion utilities. The page is a unified web+GitHub source: its companion repository archives the source code of the many Oric games and demos the OSDK produces. Together the wiki now spans the full lifecycle — the history and hardware of the Oric ([[oric.free.fr]]), the toolchain that builds software for it ([[defence-force.org]]), and the open-hardware boards that recreate the machine itself ([[OldWer-Metaphoric]], [[Board-Folk-Oric-Remix]]).

[[forum.defence-force.org]] adds the community layer. It is the phpBB discussion board of the Defence Force site — 16 sub-forums where Oric users and developers troubleshoot hardware faults, report OSDK bugs, and trade 6502 and BASIC techniques. Where the reference sources are static, the forum is the live edge of the scene: its recent threads track current OSDK releases, modern extensions such as the LOCI storage emulator and ULA-replacement concepts, and concrete repair questions. It is also a searchable archive — though the phpBB search system requires a logged-in account — making it the place to turn when a specific problem is not covered by the reference material.

[[library.defence-force.org]] is the Oric Library — a 818-entry digital archive of period books, magazines, manuals, and datasheets, maintained as part of the Defence Force family. The collection spans 16 countries and covers publications from 1978 to 2021, with 62% of entries available as downloadable PDFs. It is the single largest repository of Oric-era documentation online: 125 books (including Oric-specific programming guides and 6502 references), 606 magazine issues (national user-group newsletters in Danish, German, French, English, and more), and 54 manuals. It is where to turn for the period literature that the other wiki sources assume but do not reproduce — the original Oric BASIC manual, the user-group newsletters, and the programming books that informed the software and hardware the rest of the wiki documents.

[[osdk.org]] completes the toolchain layer by supplying the per-tool reference documentation that [[defence-force.org]] links to but does not itself contain. A dedicated documentation site for the OSDK, it covers every tool in the kit: the RCC16 C compiler (data types, optimisation flags, known limitations), the XA 6502 assembler (opcodes, directives, preprocessing), the source-level linker (command-line switches, pragma directives for localisation), FloppyBuilder (DOS-free disk image generation by integer-indexed file slots), and the full suite of tape, disk, image and audio utilities. Its companion GitHub repository holds the C source of the toolchain itself — a separate repo from the games-and-demos archive linked from [[defence-force.org]] — making [[osdk.org]] the right entry point when building the OSDK from source or tracing a tool bug to its implementation.

[[blog.defence-force.org]] is the personal blog of Mickaël Pointier (Dbug), covering Oric development and retro-computing in long-form articles from roughly 2015 to the present. Its most significant contribution is the Encounter game series: an exhaustive dev-log and post-mortem spanning a 1984 failed prototype, a 2018 BASIC reboot, and the 2024 commercial FloppyBuilder/6502 release on itch.io — the most detailed public record of shipping a modern Oric game. The blog also hosts the Oriclopedia video series (Oric hardware models, publishers including Tansoft, Loriciels, IJK, and Ere Informatique), practical floppy-emulator guides (GoTek + FlashFloppy on Microdisc), and an insider series on Adeline Software's Time Commando (level workflow, ACF/XCF resource formats, LibMenu). Since 2026 it has added articles on AI-assisted development with Claude Code, including a case study modernising the blog's own PHP engine. It is the narrative companion to the reference material at [[defence-force.org]], [[osdk.org]], and [[forum.defence-force.org]].
